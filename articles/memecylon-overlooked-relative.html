Memecylon: The Overlooked Melastome Relative

OC
Owen Castillo, Plant Ecology Writer
•June 19, 2026•6 min read

Not every genus with a strong family resemblance stays neatly filed under the family it resembles. Memecylon is a case in point. For a long time it was treated as part of Melastomataceae, and it's still discussed alongside the family constantly because of how closely related and visually similar it is — small-leaved evergreen shrubs and trees, mostly tropical, with the family's characteristic look. Depending on which classification system you consult, Memecylon is now placed either within Melastomataceae or in its own closely allied family, Memecylaceae.

This isn't a case of one authority being right and another wrong. It reflects an honest, ongoing disagreement among taxonomists about where exactly to draw a line that, at a genetic level, doesn't fall in an obviously clean place.

Why this kind of disagreement happens

Genus and family boundaries used to be drawn almost entirely from physical traits — flower structure, leaf shape, fruit type. Genetic sequencing has since revealed relationships that don't always match those older groupings. When a genus sits right at the boundary between two closely related families, different researchers can reasonably reach different conclusions about which side it belongs on, and both positions may show up in the literature simultaneously.

What Memecylon actually looks like

Practically, Memecylon species are typically small trees or shrubs with small, leathery, opposite leaves and small blue or purple flowers, widespread across tropical Asia and Africa. Several species have local uses — some yield dyes, others have a history in traditional medicine — though as with most plants in this space, that traditional use shouldn't be read as established modern medical evidence.
