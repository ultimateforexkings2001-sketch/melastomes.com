Melastomataceae: A Complete Family Overview

EM
Elena Marsh, Botanical Writer
•May 28, 2026•9 min read

Melastomataceae is one of those plant families that's enormous in scope but rarely discussed outside botanical circles. With somewhere around 150 genera and over 5,000 species, it's among the larger flowering plant families on Earth — and yet most people who've walked past a member of it in a botanical garden or bought one at a nursery have no idea it belongs to a family at all, let alone this one.

Where it sits in the plant kingdom

Melastomataceae belongs to the order Myrtales, placing it alongside families like Myrtaceae (myrtles, eucalyptus) and Onagraceae (evening primroses). Despite the shared order, Melastomataceae has a look distinctly its own, driven by a few consistent structural traits that show up across nearly the entire family.

What identifies a plant as a melastome

  • Distinctive leaf venation. Most species show several main veins running roughly parallel from the leaf base to tip, connected by finer cross-veins — a pattern that's often visible from a short distance, unlike the branching venation seen in most other plant families.
  • Flowers in multiples of four or five. Petal counts are usually consistent within a species, with stamens frequently curved or hooked in a way that's distinctive under close inspection.
  • Fruit type. Most species produce either a berry or a capsule, often containing large numbers of tiny seeds.

Where melastomes grow

The family is overwhelmingly concentrated in the tropics, with the greatest diversity found in Central and South America, though significant diversity also exists across tropical Asia and Africa. Species range from towering rainforest trees to small forest-floor herbs, with the bulk of well-known genera falling somewhere in between as shrubs and small trees.

A family this large rarely gets simple — Melastomataceae's taxonomy has been revised repeatedly as researchers apply genetic evidence to a group long classified mostly by physical appearance.

Why the family matters beyond botany

A handful of genera — Tibouchina chief among them — have crossed over into mainstream horticulture, prized for showy flowers and relatively easy care in warm climates. Others have ecological importance as food sources for tropical wildlife, or economic relevance in traditional medicine practices across their native ranges. Most of the family's species, however, remain known mainly to specialists working in the regions where they grow.

A family still being mapped

Because tropical regions are historically under-surveyed compared to temperate zones, new Melastomataceae species are still being formally described on a fairly regular basis, and existing genus boundaries continue to shift as researchers apply DNA-based classification methods. It's a useful reminder that "well-documented" and "settled" aren't the same thing, even for a family this size.

The bottom line

Melastomataceae is large, tropical, and structurally distinctive once you know what to look for — parallel leaf veins and curved stamens being the two easiest identifying traits for a non-specialist. It's a family worth knowing by name, even if most of its members will only ever be seen by the botanists who study them.
